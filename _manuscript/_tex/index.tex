\section{Specific Aims}\label{specific-aims}

\subsection{Background}\label{background}

According to National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH), 29.5
million people age 12 and older had alcohol use disorder (AUD) in the
past year, whereas only 2.2 million (less than 10\%) in this age group
received alcohol treatment\textsuperscript{1,2}. Provider availability
in rural areas, fear of stigma, and economic hardship contribute to
racial, gender and age disparities in treatment
seeking\textsuperscript{3--8}. The emergence of digital therapeutics
(DTx) has provided new opportunities to address such health disparity in
underprivileged groups with its higher accessibility, acceptability and
at least modest efficacy\textsuperscript{9--12}. The broad goal of this
program of research is to improve the effectiveness of DTx, potentially
by adding personal sensing technology and algorithms that allow DTx to
have more refined capacities in anticipating lapses and recommending
optimal treatment selection that meets individuals' momentary
needs\textsuperscript{13--15}

Machine learning models leveraging ecological momentary assessment (EMA)
measures have performed relatively well to predict alcohol relapse
risks, yet this methodology is associated with several limitations. As
it actively relies on self-reports, it brings presumably large burden to
users. This is particularly problematic as AUD is a chronic disease that
requires constant risk monitoring. Reducing sampling rates and number of
survey items might partially relieve this burden but at the cost of
impairing prediction precision and temporal specificity. Further, this
method is based on a top-down, theory-driven approach and are thus
constrained to the scope of predictor selection and subject to
limitations of previous findings. We might miss important predictors
owing to gaps in our knowledge of alcohol relapse risk factors. Models
built upon results from decades of research on White, male-dominant
samples might also even exacerbate inequity when they are applied to
DTx.

Passive sensing using text message monitoring represents new
opportunities to address the above issues while still retaining some
benefits of EMA. As text sensing collects data continuously and
automatically, it imposes lower burden on users. Research has found that
users report more willingness to use passive sensing measures for a
longer period of time\textsuperscript{16}. Some feature engineering
techniques allow researchers to mine underlying constructs of texts.
They are unconstrained and are thus less susceptible to gaps in theories
and homogeneity in previous research samples. One benefit of collecting
EMA measures is that the models are built upon robust predictors that
are supported by decades of research. Some feature engineering methods
can still seize benefits of this theory-driven approach by feeding
algorithms with well-established risk factors. For example, sentiment
analysis can reveal emotional tones, resembling subjective feelings
measures in EMA.

Text messaging analysis can be informative of both \emph{who} are at
risks of relapses and \emph{why} they relapse. Language analysis can
have high predictability with regards to alcohol-related outcomes.
Indeed, natural language processing (NLP) techniques on electronic
health records and clinical notes have demonstrated moderate performance
in predicting alcohol misuse, risky alcohol use and alcohol use
status\textsuperscript{17--21}. Further, although some NLP methods are
hard to interpret, some might have potentials to generate meaningful
findings that might optimize DTx design to customize recommended
treatment at the moment. For example, LIWC (see \emph{Section Customized
Dictionary}) might deepen our understanding of psycholinguistic risk
factors\textsuperscript{22} and sentimental analysis weighing emotional
tone might have implications for emotion-focused therapy. Topic modeling
that examines hidden semantic patterns and identifies clusters of topics
might reveal cautionary topics that trigger alcohol use.

\subsection{Current Study}\label{current-study}

In the current study, I will use different feature engineering
techniques combined with various statistical algorithms to analyze
participants' SMS messages aiming to predict their relapse risks.
Broadly, I will evaluate both the predictability and interpretability of
each distinct method. Followings are the more specific aims:

\textbf{Aim 1: Train and evaluate predictive performance of machine
learning models built upon language features derived from different NLP
techniques to predict alcohol relapse risks.} We will conduct various
feature engineering methods such as topic modeling, sentiment analysis,
bag of words, word embeddings and customized dictionary. For each
distinct feature set derived from these natural language processing
techniques, we will build machine learning models including glmnet
(penalized, parametric linear model), k-nearest neighbor (KNN),
linear/quadratic discriminant analysis (LDA/QDA), random forest (RF),
and neural network (NN).Predictability of alcohol lapse risks will be
evaluated based on prediction accuracy.

\textbf{Aim 2: Identify important features and evaluate their
interpretability with respect to recommending interventions.} Model
interpretation is key to providing treatment recommendations and
uncovering potential relapse causes. We will calculate both global
feature importance and local feature importance across all feature
engineering techniques and machine learning algorithms. We will
qualitatively assess top-performing features (i.e., with high feature
importance).

\textbf{Aim 3: Examine model fairness in historically underprivileged
subgroup populations.} It is also important to note that if embedded
algorithms perform relatively worse for marginalized groups, their use
can exacerbate rather than alleviate treatment disparities. As such,
model performance between privileged vs.~unprivileged groups should be
carefully examined. We will evaluate model performance in demographic
subgroups that face excessive barriers accessing alcohol treatments or
medications, including females, racial minorities, individuals living
under poverty, and older population. The performance metrics we choose
are area under the receiver operating characteristic (auROC), area under
the precision-recall curve (auPR), positive predictive value (PPV), true
positive rate (sensitivity), and true negative rate (specificity).

\section{Significance}\label{significance}

AUD remains a prevalent, lasting and costly problem in the United States
over the past few years. According to 2022 NSDUH\textsuperscript{1,2},
an estimate of 29.5 million (10.5\% of the population) individuals aged
12 or older had AUD in 2022, which is consistent with the estimates for
2021. AUD problems have also brought huge economic burdens to the U.S.,
where excessive alcohol use has cost \$223.5 billion in 2006 and \$249
billion in 2010\textsuperscript{23}.

Despite the high prevalence of AUD, limited number of individuals
receive alcohol treatments. Only a small portion (7.6\%) of individuals
aged 12 or older with AUD acquired alcohol use treatment in the past
year\textsuperscript{1,2}. This low treatment acquisition rate is even
more pronounced for individuals from disadvantaged groups. Disparities
in treatment-seeking behavior based on race, gender, and age are
evident\textsuperscript{3--8}. Only 6.6\% of Black or African-American
people, 3.8\% of people of two or more races, and 4.8\% of Hispanic or
Latino people with past-year AUD received
treatments\textsuperscript{1,2}. Women are less likely to obtain
treatments compared to men, even if they have equivalent level of
perceived need for help\textsuperscript{24}.

Several obstacles hinder people's utilization of treatment options.
Previous studies have identified that AUD patients face financial
barriers\textsuperscript{6,7}, lack of knowledge or
awareness\textsuperscript{25--27}, social
stigma\textsuperscript{27--30}, and geographical
barriers\textsuperscript{31} for accessing care. Individuals with
disadvantaged demographic background suffer from even heightened
barriers to treatment resources. Women with high severity of alcohol use
face larger fear of stigma compared to their male
counterparts\textsuperscript{29}.

DTx is an emerging tool that shows promising potentials in addressing
extant challenges AUD patients face in utilizing alcohol treatments. DTx
are evidence-based health software that generate or deliver medical
intervention to patients at the goal of treating, alleviating, or
managing a disease or disorder. They show effectiveness in treating
mental health conditions including AUD and demonstrate clear clinical
advantages\textsuperscript{32--34}. Further, DTx demonstrate significant
potentials in approaching hard-to-reach populations, including those
from socially marginalized group who encounter increased barriers to
access treatment. They can benefit patients from rural areas with low
provider availability as they are accessible remotely via mobile
phones\textsuperscript{10,35}. DTx also have potentials to relieve
barriers to seek professional help resulting from stigma concerns as
they can facilitate anonymity and shorten the need for in-person
interactions. They are of lower costs compared to in person care and
have the capacity to be scalable.

The concept of ``smart'' DTx is emerging in recent years allowing us to
expand the benefits of DTx. Smart DTx comprises two key components.
First, they rely on personal sensing methods to collect data. A
satisfying personal sensing technology should be 1) feasible and low
burden on users; 2) longitudinal; and 3) with high temporal granularity.
Exemplary measures include EMA, which actively prompts users to complete
surveys and can encompass desired questions on moods, social
relationships, stressful events, etc. Other less active measures include
geolocation, phone call logs and text messages collection. GPS tracking,
phone logs sharing and SMS message sharing continuously and passively
gather information. Combined with self-reports, smart DTx are capable of
acquiring contextual information such as levels of support user can
obtain from their frequently visited places and frequently contacted
people.

Second, the rich dataset extracted from personal sensing can be
incorporated into machine learning algorithms. The goal of such models
is to identify \emph{who} are at heightened risks of relapsing,
\emph{when} they will relapse, and \emph{why} they are at increased
risks. Through the embedded algorithms, smart DTx can achieve two tasks:
1) continuous AUD relapse risk-monitoring; and 2) individualized,
just-in-time treatment recommendations when needed. Notably, these
functions are very important given the dynamic nature of alcohol
relapse. AUD is a chronic disease where patients face constant
challenges of relapsing after abstinence\textsuperscript{36,37}. Indeed,
current work has expanded the relapse prevention model to reflect the
temporal, multifaceted, and dynamic process of alcohol
relapse\textsuperscript{38}.

The first question with regard to building such algorithms is concerned
with decisions on what the predicted outcome (i.e., relapse risks) can
be. A desired outcome should be clearly and precisely defined across
individuals, easy to measure, and with high temporal precision. Relapse,
which usually refers to the return of a symptomatic behavior, is hard to
quantify due to its multidimensional nature and temporal
coarseness\textsuperscript{39}. One potential conceptualization of
alcohol relapse is linked to problems of use. Nonetheless, negative
consequences are multifaceted and can therefore be burdensome to
collect. It is also unclear what the onset of problems are. Another
possible outcome is levels of alcohol use. However, this measure is not
temporally precise because there will be a time lag between onset of
drinking and the last drink completed. Levels of drinking might also
mean differently for individuals with different AUD severity, which
makes it hardly generalizable across individuals. Alternatively, in this
study, we utilize lapse (i.e., a single episode of alcohol use) as our
primary outcome variable. Lapses are easy to define, have a clear onset,
and are also clinically meaningful. They can serve as an early warning
sign of failure to sustain a desired behavioral
change\textsuperscript{40,41}. Research has also shown that initial
lapse and frequent lapses are the associated with enhanced risks of
relapses\textsuperscript{42,43}.

The next question pertains to determining what features we can input
into algorithms. To begin with, the features should be easy to measure
and feasible. The widespread availability of smartphones has rendered
constant data collection with DTx attainable. Data collection procedure
should also bring low burden to users to ensure sustainable use of smart
DTx. Previous research has established satisfying acceptability of both
active self-report measures such as EMA and passive sensing such as
geolocation sensing\textsuperscript{16}. The features should also be
well-validated. For example, GPS tracking has high accuracy in locating
individuals and is temporally precise.

Desirable features should meaningfully predict lapses. More
specifically, they should both detect \emph{who} are at increased lapse
risks and \emph{when} the lapse will occur. These predictions allow
windows for subsequent timely intervention to prevent lapses targeting a
specific person. Notably, considering the relatively lower costs
associated with the DTx than traditional in-person therapies, the
caveats of misclassifying a non-lapse as lapse might be lower than
labeling a lapse as non-lapse. Therefore, prediction accuracy on
positive classes should be carefully examined in addition to overall
performance accuracy.

We can derive top-performing features both from a top-down,
theory-driven approach and a bottom-up, empirically-driven approach. A
top-down approach involves integrating domain-specific knowledge of
known risks for alcohol lapses from a relapse prevention model into the
algorithms. Such features are multifaceted and might involve affect
state, social interactions, and alcohol-related factors. For instance,
the majority of an inpatient teen sample reported initial relapse to
alcohol when offered alcohol, when in negative state, and when in
interpersonal conflicts\textsuperscript{44}. Other commonly found risk
factors include alcohol craving\textsuperscript{45,46}, negative affect
state\textsuperscript{45}, cognitive factors\textsuperscript{45}, and
interpersonal problem\textsuperscript{45}. Contrarily, a bottom-up
approach starts with collecting data from various sources and then
analyzes them at a granular level and extracts relevant features.

Features should also have temporal granularity given the dynamic nature
of alcohol relapse risks. Smart DTx algorithms with good temporal
precision should consistently collect data on situated, risk-relevant
predictors while retaining low burden on users. Exemplary features with
good temporal precision might involve the proximal factors depicted in
the relapse prevention model\textsuperscript{38,47,48}. Proximal factors
(e.g., drinking partner, drinking behavior) are situated and can be
fluctuating over time. They usually occurs before an episode of alcohol
use\textsuperscript{49}. They can be elusive to therapists' attention
due to infrequent visits, highlighting importance of sustained
monitoring to prevent lapses.

Useful risk prediction algorithms are capable of identifying
unrecognized risk factors for lapses. The bottom-up approach does not
pre-specify indexes when building models. This method is therefore less
susceptible to our knowledge gaps and might even discover previously
unfound underlying processes contributing to alcohol lapses. We might
even expect less differential performance under the bottom up approach
across privileged vs.~unprivileged groups because the features are not
drawn from past research dominated by White males with AUD and are thus
potentially unbiased towards privileged groups.

Smart DTx algorithms should also incorporate features that are
interpretable and can map on to current interventions. Exemplary
features that are highly interpretable include those derived from the
top-down, theory-driven approach. These features are easy to interpret
and closely align with established therapies. For example, features
related to social relationship might have important implications for
family or marital counseling. Affect state features might be informative
of emotion-focused therapy. In addition to selecting interpretable
features during data training, computational methods can also be used to
enhance model interpretability by analyzing feature importance. For
instance, we can examine global feature importance to determine which
features contribute the most to predict lapses across individuals.
Further, examining local feature importance (i.e., features influencing
a single observation) in these models might also be helpful for model
interpretation. They have benefits of identifying the factors that
contribute to lapse risk for any specific person and moment in time.

Importantly, the selected features should also be unbiased towards
disadvantaged groups. One of the key benefits of DTx is that they
partially address barriers utilizing professional help by providing
24/7/365, affordable, personalized support. They are particularly
beneficial to marginalized groups who have low rates of utilizing
treatment options due to these treatment barriers. However, they might
exacerbate health inequity if embedded algorithms perform relatively
worse for less privileged groups. One possible explanation of
differential performance across subgroups might stem from the features
we feed the algorithms. For example, features derived from a
theory-driven approach might be biased towards advantaged groups because
they depend on decades of research on White males. The biased features
therefore might favor better prediction performance for advantaged
groups because they are more robust in predicting their relapse risks.
Notably, the differential performance between different groups might
also be owing to the under-representation of unprivileged groups in the
dataset besides feature selection. The algorithms might sacrifice some
prediction accuracy for the minority groups if they are severely
underrepresented in the data to ensure overall model performance. As
such, it is important to recruit a diverse sample to complete model
training. Some statistical resampling strategy might also be used to
increase the representation of marginalized groups in the dataset.

Recent DTx work has yielded satisfactory performance in predicting
lapses with EMA measures. Our group developed an XGBoost machine
learning model built upon self-reported craving, affect, efficacy, risky
situations, stressful events, pleasant events to predict alcohol lapses
in the next hour, day, or week\textsuperscript{50}. The surveys were
collected up to four times daily for three months. The model achieved
exceptional performance when predicting lapses for new individuals, with
a mean auROC score of .89, .90 and .93 for the hour-, day-, and
week-level model respectively. Global feature importance demonstrated
that past alcohol use and future self-efficacy consistently contributed
greatly across all models to predict lapses.

Nonetheless, relying on EMA measures for model building is associated
with several limitations. First, constantly completing surveys makes it
burdensome for real-world DTx use. Although most EMA relevant mental
health research demonstrated modest compliance rates, their time windows
last from two weeks to three months\textsuperscript{51--55}. The study
length is insufficient for real-world DTx use. As extended period of
time of app use is anticipated, users' perceived burden of answering
surveys is presumably larger\textsuperscript{56}. Although minimizing
the number of items in the surveys and the frequency of prompting users
to complete the surveys might help mitigate the associated burden, it
can inevitably reduce the prediction precision and temporal precision of
algorithms.

Second, decisions regarding what constructs to assess and what items to
include to assess these constructs are limited by theory and past data.
Including risk factors solely drawn from decades of research on White,
male-dominant samples might even exacerbate health disparities when
applied to DTx. Further, our current understanding of alcohol relapse
precursors is not comprehensive, especially for proximal factors. For
example, Marlatt's proposed taxonomy characterizes high-risk situational
precursors to alcohol relapse such as social pressure and
positive/negative emotional state\textsuperscript{57}. Nonetheless,
replication studies have found this theoretical framework to be somewhat
unreliable and have low predictive validity of post-treatment
outcomes\textsuperscript{58--60}. A review study also suggests that
current proximal factors are not well-understood in past research due to
methodological constraints and a death of ``near real-time''
data\textsuperscript{47}.

Text sensing technology, which is both feasible and sustainable,
represents new opportunities in DTx that might address limitations of
the current active reporting approach. Since AUD is a chronic condition
requiring ongoing risk monitoring over an extended period, DTx can
benefit from SMS sensing because it places a low burden on users and
allows for continuous data collecti138on. Studies collecting passive
data have demonstrated high acceptability from participants and higher
compliance rates compared to active measures\textsuperscript{9,16}.
Further, risk monitoring using SMS sensing is temporally sensitive to
fluctuating risks. Analyzing text messages can detect potential triggers
in time without actively prompting users to reflect on their feelings at
the moment or report their environment.

With the burgeoning of NLP techniques, language analysis is
well-equipped with diverse feature engineering tools to have good
predictability with temporal specificity for alcohol lapses in new
individuals. Some top-down methods might have good performance because
they allow researchers to mine previously established robust construct
of alcohol lapse precursors. For example, a bag-of-words approach using
customized dictionary might allow researchers to mine alcohol-related
content from messages such as alcohol cravings. Sentiment analysis can
also enable researchers to assess individuals' affect state. Further,
some NLP methods follow a bottom-up approach. They might identify new
alcohol lapse precursors and are less susceptible to limitations in past
research. For example, topic modeling techniques such as Latent
Dirichlet Analysis (LDA) and Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) can examine
patterns in SMS messages and identify clusters of topics from the data.
Word embeddings approach transforms words into vectors with high
dimension space.

NLP also offers avenues for model interpretation to yield valuable
insights into treatment recommendations. Although some feature
engineering techniques (e.g., word embeddings) might reveal arbitrary
features that is incomprehensible, some can still mine features that can
be potentially interpretable (e.g., topic clustering). Assessing their
feature importance helps us understand how features contribute to the
models (i.e., which features are robust in predicting lapses). For
example, global feature importance can identify robust predictors across
individuals in predicting lapses. Local feature importance provides
insights on what contributes to a lapse for a specific person at a
specific time. They can be useful in personalized, just-in-time
treatment recommendations.

\subsection{Current Study}\label{current-study-1}

In this study, we will utilize different NLP techniques to predict
lapses from SMS message content. More specifically, we will compare
across different feature engineering methods encompassing topic
modeling, sentiment analysis, bag-of-words, word embeddings, and
customized dictionary. We will evaluate model performance from three
aspects: 1) overall model prediction performance in detecting lapses; 2)
model interpretability; and 3) performance between advantaged groups
vs.~disadvantaged groups with regards to race, gender, income and age.

\section{Approach}\label{approach}

\subsection{Overview}\label{overview}

This study will analyze data collected from 2017-2019 from a larger
grant funded by National Institute of Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (R01
AA024391). It is important to note that additional measures were
collected from the participants that are not directly related to this
study. All materials are described at https://osf.io/w5h9y/.

\subsection{Participants}\label{participants}

Individuals at early stages of recovery from AUD were recruited from
Madison and surrounding area via social media platforms (e.g.,
Facebook), referrals from clinics, and television and radio
advertisements. After initial phone screen, interested individuals came
in-person to complete a more in-depth screening to determine their
eligibility. We documented their demographic information. Inclusion
criteria include that participants: 1) must be at least aged 18 or
older; 2) must meet criteria for AUD with at least moderate severity
(\textgreater four DSM-5 criteria); 3) must be abstinent from alcohol
for at least one week and fewer than two months at time of intake; 4)
must be able to read and write in English; 5) must be willing to use
smartphone and their smartphone is compatible with our study technology.
Participants were excluded if they have a lifetime history of severe and
persistent mental illness. One hundred sixty-nine participants were
eligible and enrolled in the study. After excluding participants who
discontinued before the first follow-up session and those with low
compliance rates, we have a final sample size of 151 participants.

\subsection{Procedures}\label{procedures}

The study lasted up to three months with five in-person visits (see
\emph{Figure 1}). Participants completed an in-person screening visit to
determine their eligibility, obtain their informed consent, and collect
their demographic information and self-report measures. They then
completed an intake session one week later and three follow-up visits
afterwards spaced at one-month intervals. At intake, participants
reported their frequently contacted individuals and their drinking
status and levels of support to participants' recovery. During each of
the follow-up visits, a research assistant downloaded participants' SMS
messages from their phone. Additional self-reported measures were
obtained (see https://osf.io/w5h9y/). Throughout the course of the
study, participants were expected to complete four daily EMAs that asked

\subsubsection{SMS Messages}\label{sms-messages}

At each of the follow-up visit, a research assistant downloaded the
participants' SMS messages from their phone. Measures included the
message type (incoming vs.~outgoing), date and time sent/received, text
body, contact name, whether the participants read the text or not, etc.
Images and voice texts were excluded from analysis. Both group messages
and one-on-one messages were obtained from participants' phones.

\subsubsection{Alcohol Lapses}\label{alcohol-lapses}

Participants were prompted up to four times daily to report their recent
alcohol use. In the first item of each daily EMA survey, dates and times
of any unreported past alcohol use were obtained. Reports of past
alcohol use is used as a dichotomous outcome variable (Lapse vs.~No
Lapse). We will predict alcohol lapses in the next 24-hour window (i.e.,
next day lapse prediction). Predictions are updated every hour so that
all algorithms will provide hour-by-hour estimation. The first
prediction window will start from the midnight of the second day of
participation and end 24 hours later.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}\label{feature-engineering}

Text messages will be the sole predictor in the analysis. We will use
several natural language processing techniques to transform SMS messages
into machine-interpretable data and input them into machine learning
algorithms. The methods we consider include: 1) topic modeling; 2)
sentiment analysis; 3) bag-of-words; 4) word embeddings; and 5)
customized dictionary. Each feature engineering technique will be
evaluated separately.

Note that these feature engineering techniques encompass both top-down
approaches and bottom-up approaches. Topic modeling, n-grams, and word
embeddings are exemplary empirically-driven methods. Sentiment analysis
aligns with concepts in affect science. LIWC relies on theories broadly
in psycho-social research, and customized dictionary can be refined to
narrower domain knowledge specific to addiction science. Features built
from these top-down approaches might be slightly more interpretable and
can map on to current interventions.

\subsubsection{Topic Modeling}\label{topic-modeling}

Topic modeling is an unsupervised machine learning method to identify
clusters of topics in a body of text. In this study, I will use one of
the most popular topic modeling technique -- Latent Dirichlet Analysis
(LDA). LDA unravels the hidden structure in the relationships between
words in a document and groups similar words into topics. It assumes
that different documents (i.e., SMS messages) have same topic clusters
but with different distribution. As such, different topic clusters might
have common words.

\subsubsection{Sentiment Analysis}\label{sentiment-analysis}

Research has found that positive/negative emotional state to be
associated with increased alcohol relapse
risks\textsuperscript{44,45,47}. Sentiment Analysis infers the emotional
underpinning of texts. We will extract both emotional valence (i.e.,
positive, neutral, and negative emotional tone) and eight basic emotion
categories (e.g., anger, fear, anticipation, trust, surprise, sadness,
joy, and disgust) from SMS messages. We will assign each SMS message a
score in those ten categories by summing sentiment content of all words
in that message.

\subsubsection{Bag-of-words Approach}\label{bag-of-words-approach}

A bag-of-words approach represents word occurrences inside a document. I
will first extract one-gram and two-gram words from the messages. Then I
will analyze these n-grams based on their counts, binary representaiton,
term frequency (tf), and inverse document frequency (idf). Counts
represent the number of occurrences of a specific n-gram in a single
text. Binary representation implies whether that n-gram is present in
the text.

I will also analyze tf, which indicates the frequency of the n-grams in
each individual text relative to that text length. I will also further
utilize combination of both tf and idf to calculate tf-idf, which
indicates word importance inside a document (see below formula). This
scoring method has the advantage of highlighting the importance of a
word to a document in a collection of documents. It has important
implications in analyzing SMS messages so that we have a better
understanding of the robustness of a certain word to a single message
across all text messages that the participants sent. The idf score
minimizes weights of words that are commonly used across all messages
and can highlight words that contain important information.

\[
\text{tf} = \frac{n_{terms\ in\ document}}{n_{terms}}
\] \[
\text{idf} = \ln(\frac{n_{documents}}{n_{documents\ containing\ term}})
\] \[
\text{tf-idf} = tf * idf
\]

\paragraph{Customized Dictionary}\label{customized-dictionary}

This study will also expand above bags-of-word approach by using
customized dictionaries built upon the LIWC
dictionary\textsuperscript{61} and compute score by counting frequency
of words that belong to each category. LIWC aligns with the current
alcohol relapse risk factor literature in that it examines psychometric
properties including cognitive state and social
processes\textsuperscript{44,45,45}. In addition to these linguistic
categories, we will also include customized alcohol-related category as
predictors as previous research has found them to be robust indicators
of alcohol lapses\textsuperscript{45,46}.

\subsubsection{Word Embeddings}\label{word-embeddings}

Word embeddings are vectors that represent relationships of words within
a document in a lower-dimension. I will use the pre-trained
Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers
(BERT)\textsuperscript{62} to transform SMS messages into such
machine-readable vectors. They are capable of capturing embedded
semantic structure within a neural network architecture. Compared to
traditional word embeddings methods (e.g., Word2Vec or GloVe), they take
into account the context of where a word is (i.e., word tokens before
and after the target word). It is also pre-trained on a massive dataset,
which allows the model to learn a wide range of patterns and structures.

\subsection{Candidate Machine Learning Algorithms and Model
Training}\label{candidate-machine-learning-algorithms-and-model-training}

We consider a range of candidate algorithms that vary with regard to
their model complexity, model assumptions, and training speed. Candidate
algorithms that will be used include glmnet (penalized, parametric
linear model), k-nearest neighbor (KNN), linear/quadratic discriminant
analysis (LDA/QDA), random forest (RF), neural network (NN), and
XGBoost. We will perform a grouped, nested cross-validation to perform
hyperparameter tuning and select the best model configuration. The
dataset will be participant-grouped so that each individual will be
assigned to either held-in or held-out set to avoid bias of predicting
participants' lapses using their own data. The nested cross-validation
method uses two nested loops to divide folds. In the inner loop,
held-out folds are used as a validation set for model selection. In the
outer loop, held-out folds are utilized as a test set for model
evaluation. More specifically, we will use one repeat of 10-fold
cross-validation in the inner loop for model selection and 3 repeats of
10-fold cross-validation in outer loop for model evaluation.

\subsection{Model Performance Evaluation and Feature
Importance}\label{model-performance-evaluation-and-feature-importance}

The primary performance metrics to select the best model configuration
and evaluate the model performance on the test sets will be auROC. auROC
computes the area under the ROC curve which demonstrates the trade-off
between sensitivity and specificity across all possible thresholds. We
choose this metric because it is more informative than accuracy for
imbalanced data. Values between .70 and .80 are considered fair, values
between .80 and .90 are considered good, and values above .90 are
considered excellent. We will report the median auROCs for the 30
held-out folds. We will further perform a Bayesian hierarchical
generalized linear model to estimate the posterior probability
distributions of the auROCs and determine if they are meaningfully
different than .50.

Note that our models will output predicted probabilities of each
observation falling into lapse vs.~no-lapse class. Our primary model
evaluation metrics (auROC) indicate the ability of the models to
distinguish between two classes across all probability thresholds. We
will also conduct supplemental performance evaluation using other
metrics and class prediction at a specific probability threshold. Binary
classification (Lapse vs.~No Lapse) will be determined with optimal
decision threshold determined by Youden's Index. We will report
sensitivity, specificity, balanced accuracy, positive predictive value
(PPV), and negative predictive value (NPV) at this threshold.

We will also calculate SHAP values to interpret the results. SHAP is a
game theory based method to explain how each feature influences the
model output. It assigns importance to each feature, where a positive
feature importance positively affects the model output. This methodology
can be applied to any machine learning algorithm and can increase model
transparency and interpretability.

\subsection{Algorithmic Bias
Evaluation}\label{algorithmic-bias-evaluation}

In addition to auROC, we will also evaluate auPRC, sensitivity,
specificity and PPV across different subgroups. NPV is not included due
to the highly imbalanced outcomes in our dataset. We will compare these
performance metrics between different sex, race, income and age
subgroups as these demographic minority groups face enhanced barriers to
access alcohol treatment due to stigma, financial difficulties, etc.
They are also traditionally underrepresented in research settings.
